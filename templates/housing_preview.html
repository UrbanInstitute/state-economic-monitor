Housing

Assessing quarterly state house prices data compared with one year earlier and the national peak (first quarter of 2007).
Roll over the bar charts, scatter plots, maps, and legends to see additional data.

According to the Federal Housing Finance Agency House Price Index, national house prices in the second quarter of 2019 were 5.0 percent higher than in the second quarter of 2018, with house prices increasing over the year in all 50 states and the District of Columbia.

Over the year, the largest house price increases were in Idaho (11.4 percent), Utah (7.7 percent), and Tennessee (7.2 percent). In total, 24 states saw house prices increase more than 5.0 percent. The smallest price increases were in Delaware (1.2 percent), Maryland (1.5 percent), and the District of Columbia (1.8 percent). All other states saw house price increases of more than 2.0 percent over the year.

Before the Great Recession, national house prices peaked in the first quarter of 2007. In 2016, national prices recovered from the recession and surpassed their 2007 peak, but prices in some states remain at or below their pre-recession levels.

In the second quarter of 2019, national house prices were 21.1 percent above the prerecession peak. Among the states, house prices were above their first-quarter 2007 levels in 45 states and the District of Columbia. Colorado saw the largest increase (74.1 percent), followed by the District of Columbia (68.8 percent), Texas (61.1 percent), and North Dakota (59.3 percent). No other state saw prices increase more than 50.0 percent, but 24 additional states saw house prices more than 20.0 percent above their first-quarter 2007 levels.

House prices in the second quarter of 2019 were below their prerecession levels in five states: Connecticut (-10.1 percent), Maryland (-5.8 percent), New Jersey (-5.4 percent), Delaware (-4.0 percent), and Illinois (-2.6 percent).
